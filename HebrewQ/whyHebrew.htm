My collection of different bibles in English started in 1980 (a short 35 years ago). Some
years no bibles were added but some years many bibles were added to the collection. My
reasoning was to enhance my understanding of the bible in general by getting many
different wordings or renderings from many different translations of one particular
bible verse at a time, randomly as they came up in my bible research.

After some time I realized that what I was really after was different wordings of the
scriptures, not just different translations. Many new translations come out first with the
Christian Greek portion and that is followed by the Hebrew Scriptures a few years later.
But in those few years they might update the Greek text wording as well. Instead of
discarding the NT when I got the whole bible (NT & OT), I now checked the two NTs to see
if they were different.

An example is the relatively new 'Voice' translation:
The Voice New Testament 2006, 2007, 2008 Ecclesia Bible Society, followed by
The Voice translation 2012 Ecclesia Bible Society four years later.
2008: Mark 12:1  ...He planted and staked the grapes;...
2012: Mark 12:1  That entire phrase, above, was deleted.
I'm not trying to find fault with any translation, I'm just saying THERE ARE DIFFERENCES
and I would like to have each and everyone of these differences for my own edification.

I relish every translation or new wording of the holy scripture I can get my hands on.

CROSS-CULTURAL vs. INTERCULTURAL
’CROSS-CULTURAL’ means a comparison and contrast between two cultural groups. 
For example, my cross-cultural study of Brazilians and Mexicans 
when they celebrate a birthday shows that Mexicans love to focus on cooking 
and sharing of the food, while Brazilians love the dancing –even grandmas are dancing the samba. 

‘Intercultural’ refers to what happens when people from these two groups come together. 
As a Mexican, I may complain that there’s not enough food, 
but I love the dancing and join the group. 
Thus, INTERCULTURAL is what happens when the two (or more) culturally-different groups come together, 
interact and communicate. Both terms describe important aspects of the study.
